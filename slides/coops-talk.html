Co-ops

What's a co-op?

A cooperative business is a type of organisation that is both owned and controlled by its members

- What makes co-ops unique is that they are not run by institutional investors or distant shareholders - Can range from multi-billion pound businesses (John Lewis, Central England Cooperative) to small community enterprises - Healthcare to housing, renewables to retail, sports to social care - Co-operatives contribute Â£36.1 billion a year to the UK economy (from Co-operatives UK) - 13.1 million (of 66 million) people own the UK's co-operatives - There are many types of co-ops - owned by the people working there (worker cooperatives) - people buying the co-ops's goods/services (consumer cooperatives) - people collaborating to process / market their products (producer cooperatives) - groups who unite to enhance purchasing power (purchasing cooperatives)

How does a co-op differ from a corporation?

- Corporation is a legal entity owned by its shareholders - A corporation enjoys the rights and responsibilities that individuals possess - They can enter contracts, loan / borrow money, sue and be sued, hire employees, own assets, pay taxes - You can think of a corporation as a 'legal person'

(non-cooperative) corporations exist to maximize shareholder wealth.

- Accountable to the majority shareholders, votes are based on the shares held (co-ops: one member, one vote) - Shares are often traded (co-ops generally not)

cooperative corporations

- Are generally community-based, giving those whom the entity serves or employs a direct say in the operation of the entity - Cooperatives limit the power that individual stakeholders can wield in the cooperative (goes back to a member owning a single vote)

Why are cooperatives so great?

- Combats powerlessness by giving people some control of the businesses they are closest to - They may shop at them, work at them, supply them - Ex. farmers strengthen their position in competitive global markets - Employees can have a say in how their organisations are run - Customers can influence the businesses that they use

History of the Co-op Movement

'Anti-Mill' flour mill in Hull 1795

- Many small cooperative ventures started to spring up around the late 1700s - The 'anti-mill' flour mill in Hull is an example of an early English co-op venture - Set up by local people who were angry at the high prices charged by commercial millers

Owenism

- Utopian socialist philosophy of the 19th century social reformer Robert Owen - forerunner / grandfather of the cooperative movement - The Owenist movement created several utopian experiments prioritizing cooperative, communitarian principles in community development - closely associated to the British trade union movement

Fair exchange

Just price

Right to charity

- Rooted in 17th century English 'moral economy' - Began as a scheme to place the unemployed poor in newly built rural communities - The plan was eventually popularized by a number of Irish and English trade unionists - Many 'back-to-the-land' Owenite cooperative communities sprung up

The Seven Rochdale principles

- Brings us to 1844 when the Equitable Pioneers co-operative society in Rochdale formed - Forefathers of the Co-op groups - Opened a shop on Toad Lane in Rochdale, which is now accepted as the start of the modern co-op movement - Popularized the concept of profits being returned to the members in proportion to the purchases they make *The Divi*
- A bit of propaganda, but interesting as context to a modern cooperative corporation (and where the 7 principles are implemented in a modern sense)

voluntary and open membership

- Co-operatives are voluntary organisations, open to all persons able to use their services and willing to accept responsibilities of membership, without gender, social, racial, political or religious discrimination.

democratic member control

- Co-operatives are democratic organisations controlled by their members, who actively participate in setting their policies and making decisions. - Men and women serving as elected representatives are accountable to the membership. - In primary co-operatives members have equal voting rights (one member, one vote), and co operatives at other levels are also organised in a democratic manner.

member economic participation

- Members contribute equitably to, and democratically control, the capital of their co-operative. - At least part of that capital is usually the common property of the co-operative. - Members usually receive limited compensation, if any, on capital subscribed as a condition of membership. - Members allocate surpluses for any of the following purposes: developing their co-operative, possibly by setting up reserves, part of which at least would be indivisible; benefiting members in proportion to their transactions with the co-operative; and supporting other activities approved by the membership.

autonomy and independence

- Co-operatives are autonomous, self-help organisations controlled by their members. - If they enter into agreements with other organisations, including governments, or raise capital from external sources, they do so on terms that ensure democratic control by their members and maintain their co-operative autonomy.

education, training, and information

- Co-operatives provide education and training for their members, elected representatives, managers and employees so they can contribute effectively to the development of their co-operatives. - They inform the general public â particularly young people and opinion leaders â about the nature and benefits of co-operation.

co-operation among co-operatives

- Co-operatives serve their members most effectively and strengthen the Co-operative Movement by working together through local, national, regional and international structures.

concern for community

- Co-operatives work for the sustainable development of their communities through policies approved by their members.
